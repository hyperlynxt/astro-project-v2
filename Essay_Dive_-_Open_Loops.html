The Alienating Cost of Being Original

On what it means to grow up too different — and how the gap between understanding and agreement becomes the loneliest distance.

Published March 2024
·
Analytical Lens Phenomenological
Central Argument ?

Being original is not a social asset — it is a social tax. The further your thinking strays from the normative frame, the more you are disliked not for what you say, but for what you are. The real cost is not isolation. It is the impossibility of deep belonging.

I. The Alien in the Room

The reality of growing up profoundly different is not adequately described by the word "lonely." Loneliness implies an absence of people. What I'm describing is a presence of people who, structurally, cannot reach you. Nobody understands you — not your parents, not your siblings, not your friends, not the internet, not the films. Not their version of love, of money, of time, of the future.

This is the alienating experience: not that you don't connect, but that the gap between you and others is so structural, so dimensional, that it functions less like a social difficulty and more like a category error. You are not failing to belong. You are belonging to a category that isn't available in your environment.

Most people don't understand all the layers they share with society — because they built themselves alongside it, or because they absorbed its frame without noticing. They think love is roses and kisses. They think you have to say hello before you speak to someone. They think profound questions are somehow impolite. All of that invisible normative scaffolding makes connection possible — for them. And impossible — for you.

II. Understanding vs. Agreement

There is a mistake most people make when they feel misunderstood: they confuse it with disagreement. They say "you don't understand me" when what they mean is "you disagree with me." These are radically different claims.

"The problem is not whether we agree. The problem is whether you have dismantled enough of your frame to see what I'm actually saying."

When someone grows up highly divergent, they actually tend to understand neurotypical people quite well — you have to, to survive in their world. You've spent years translating yourself into legible formats. The asymmetry runs the other way: a very neurotypical person, embedded in the frame they were raised in, has rarely had cause to dismantle it. They don't see the frame. They are the frame.

This is why the sentence "I understand you, I just disagree" is so often the honest one from me. And why the equivalent sentence from the other side rarely lands.

III. The Social Tax of Originality

Here is something I've understood slowly: being original makes you less likeable. Not for any reason that has to do with your character. Not because you're unkind, or rude, or wrong. But because you hold a different opinion from what others are, were raised as, and identify with. And people don't just disagree with different opinions — they take them as a kind of aggression.

The strange thing is that being unusual is socially penalized even when it is delivered perfectly. The right tone, the right moment, the right words — it doesn't matter. If the content is genuinely different from the normative frame, the reaction is often hostility. Not to what you said, but to what you represent: evidence that the frame is not universal.

Normativity polices itself this way. Rules that begin as stabilizing — be considerate, don't harm others — gradually become expansive, until they absorb all the space that authenticity, creativity, and genuine thought once occupied. At some point there is no longer room to exist differently.

IV. Superficial vs. Deep Belonging

I could belong superficially. I know how to do it. I can be inauthentic, can flatten myself into the expected shape, can answer the expected questions with expected answers. I've done it. It works, in the sense that people like you more. But it isn't what I'm looking for.

What I want is deep belonging: to be understood and accepted at the level of how I actually think. That requires something from the other side that most people haven't developed — not intelligence alone, but a lack of ego, a genuine curiosity, a willingness to follow a thought into unfamiliar territory without needing to win.

The hard truth is that once you decide to actually be yourself — to think your own thoughts, communicate your own way, live outside the normative frame — that version of deep belonging stops being reliably available. You gain something rare and real when you find it. But you lose the guarantee of it. That is the trade.

Started from a voice note on the bus — the Unabomber thought came first, which surprised me. The sociological framing came later.

Still uncertain whether "deep belonging" is something that can be found, or whether wanting it is itself part of the condition.
