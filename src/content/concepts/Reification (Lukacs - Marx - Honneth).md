**Reification** is the process by which something that is actually alive, relational, historical, and human-made gets treated — and eventually *experienced* — as a fixed, static thing with its own independent existence. It is the transformation of processes into objects, of relations into facts, of the living into the frozen. **Lukács** took Marx's concept of commodity fetishism and generalized it into a total theory of modern consciousness. When you buy a product, the social relationships that produced it — the labor, the decisions, the human activity — are completely invisible. You just see a thing with a price. The price appears as a natural property of the object, like its weight or color. But it isn't — it's a *crystallized social relation* made to look like a natural fact. That's reification at the economic level.

But Lukács argued it goes much deeper than economics. When reification becomes the **dominant mode of experience** in a society, people start applying it everywhere. Social relationships get experienced as fixed and thing-like. Human nature gets experienced as static and given. History gets experienced as a natural force rather than as something humans are making. Possibilities that are actually open get experienced as closed, because they appear as natural facts rather than historical constructions *that could be otherwise*. This is also a **phenomenological** transformation — not just an intellectual error. A reified world *feels* heavy, fixed, inevitable. Change feels impossible not because someone has argued you out of it, but because the world genuinely appears as immovable. This is why reification is so politically significant: a reified consciousness cannot imagine alternatives, not because it lacks information, but because its basic mode of experiencing reality **forecloses them**.

**Honneth** added a relational dimension, arguing that the deepest form of reification is interpersonal — when you stop experiencing other people as subjects with inner lives and start experiencing them as *objects with properties*. He connected this to recognition theory: genuine human engagement requires a prior attitude of care and acknowledgment of the other as alive and real. Reification is the **forgetting of this prior recognition**. And crucially, you can reify *yourself* — treating your own capacities, desires, and possibilities as fixed properties rather than as living potentials.
