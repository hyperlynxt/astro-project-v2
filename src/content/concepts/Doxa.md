**Doxa** is the set of assumptions so deep and so naturalized that they don't appear as assumptions at all — they appear as *reality itself*, as just "the way things are." Bourdieu distinguished three zones: **Doxa** — what goes completely without saying, what is never even questioned because it doesn't appear as a position at all, just as reality; **Orthodoxy** — the dominant explicit position, what you have to actively defend; and **Heterodoxy** — the challenger position, what has to argue against the dominant view.

Most ideological analysis focuses on *orthodoxy vs. heterodoxy* — the fight between explicit positions. But Bourdieu argued that the **real power** operates at the level of doxa, because whatever enters doxa *wins completely*. It stops being a position and becomes reality.

A concrete example: for most of human history, male authority in the household was *doxic* — it wasn't a position anyone defended because it wasn't perceived as a position at all, just as how families are. The moment feminism forced it into public debate, it became **orthodoxy** — now it had to be defended, which meant it was *already losing ground*, because now it was visible as a choice rather than a natural fact.
