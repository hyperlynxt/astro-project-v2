**Social Ontology** asks what kinds of things exist *because humans collectively treat them as existing*. Money is the classic example — a dollar bill is physically just paper. It only *is* money because enough people operate as if it's money. The same logic applies to institutions, laws, marriages, borders, and corporations: none of these exist in nature, but they are *completely real* in their effects.

John Searle called these **"institutional facts"** — things that exist because of **collective human agreement** — as opposed to **"brute facts"** like rocks and gravity, which exist independently of what anyone thinks about them.

The key move of social ontology is the separation between *what physically exists* and *what socially exists*. And social existence is often **more powerful in practice** than physical existence.
