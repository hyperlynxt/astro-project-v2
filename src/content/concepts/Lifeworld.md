**The Lifeworld** (*Lebenswelt*) is the pre-theoretical, taken-for-granted horizon of meaning and experience within which all conscious life occurs — *the world as it is lived before science, theory, or reflection gets to it*. Husserl introduced the concept as a corrective to what he saw as a fundamental mistake of modern science: treating the abstract, mathematized world of physics as the real world, and treating lived experience as secondary or derivative. He argued the opposite — the lifeworld is **primary**. Science is built on top of it, abstracts from it, but can never fully replace it or account for it. What the lifeworld contains are shared meanings, familiar objects, social roles, common sense, the sense that the floor is solid and other people are conscious and that the sun will rise tomorrow. None of this is theorized — *it's just the water you swim in*.

**Schutz** extended this socially, showing that the lifeworld is fundamentally *intersubjective* — not a private world, but a shared world built up through generations of common experience, language, and practice. **When you share a lifeworld with someone, communication is effortless because you don't have to explain your basic assumptions — they're already shared.** When lifeworlds diverge, communication becomes *genuinely difficult* not because people are stupid or malicious, but because the basic taken-for-granted background is different.

**Habermas** added a critical dimension: in modernity, the lifeworld is being progressively **colonized by systems** — especially economic and administrative systems — that operate through money and power rather than through shared meaning and communication. The lifeworld runs on *mutual understanding*. Systems run on *strategic rationality*.
