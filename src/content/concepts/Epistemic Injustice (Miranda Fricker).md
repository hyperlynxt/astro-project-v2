**Epistemic Injustice** is a specific kind of wrong done to someone in their capacity as a *knower* — as someone who has knowledge, gives testimony, and makes sense of their own experience. Fricker identified two primary forms. **Testimonial injustice** occurs when someone's word is given less credibility than it deserves due to identity prejudice. A Black man reporting a crime, a woman reporting workplace harassment, a working class person making a diagnosis about their own health — in each case their testimony is systematically discredited not because of its content but because of *who they are*. The prejudice operates at the level of credibility assessment, often unconsciously: the hearer doesn't think "I'm discounting this because of their race or gender" — they just *intuitively find the testimony less convincing*.

**Hermeneutical injustice** is deeper and more structurally significant. It occurs when the collective conceptual vocabulary is so underdeveloped in some area that people **cannot make sense of their own experience** — not because they lack intelligence, but because *the concepts don't exist yet*. Fricker's example is sexual harassment before that term existed. Women were experiencing something real, harmful, and systematic — but they had no concept for it. They couldn't name it to themselves, couldn't communicate it to others, couldn't fully understand their own experience. The harm was compounded because the experience was *hermetically sealed* — trapped, uninterpretable, isolating.

This isn't random. Hermeneutical injustice **systematically affects marginalized groups** because the people who have most power over developing collective conceptual resources tend not to share those experiences. The concepts get developed for the experiences that the powerful have and find interesting. Everything else remains in a zone of **hermeneutical darkness** — real, present, and harmful, but without the language to become visible.
