**Alienation** is the condition of being separated from something that is *essentially yours* — your labor, your product, your fellow humans, your own nature — in a way that makes what is yours appear as foreign, hostile, or belonging to someone else. Most people know alienation as a vague feeling of disconnection or not fitting in. The philosophical concept is much more specific and much more structural.

**Marx** identified four dimensions. First, **alienation from the product** — you make something and it immediately belongs to someone else, confronting you as an alien power. Second, **alienation from the act of production** — work doesn't express you, it drains you; it's something you do to survive, not something that realizes your potential. Third, **alienation from species-being** — humans are unique in that we can consciously shape the world according to ideas, producing freely and creatively. Alienated labor *inverts* this: instead of labor being the expression of human freedom, it becomes a mere means of physical survival, no different from an animal feeding itself. Fourth, **alienation from other humans** — because your relationship to your own labor and product is alienated, your relationship to other people becomes mediated by those alienated categories: competition, exchange, use.

**Fromm** extended this psychologically, arguing that alienation isn't just an economic condition but a *mode of experience* — a way of relating to the world where you experience yourself and others as things rather than as living processes. The alienated person doesn't feel like the **author of their own acts** — they feel like *spectators of themselves*. Their emotions don't feel like their emotions, their thoughts don't feel like their thoughts. There's a fundamental disconnection between the self and its own experience.

The **Hegelian root** goes deeper still. For Hegel, alienation is what happens when something that is actually part of you confronts you *as if it were independent, external, and other*. Institutions and cultural forms are created by human activity — they are expressions of human spirit — but over time they solidify and appear as external constraints imposed from outside. **The thing you made starts to rule you.** This is the structure of alienation at its most abstract — and also its most recognizable.
